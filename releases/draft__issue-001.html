AI Models

From Benchmarks to Workflows: The Next Generation of Agentic AI

Agnes Lazo  · Published May 13, 2026  · 2 min read

On April 16, 2026, the AI landscape shifted with the release of Anthropic’s Claude Opus 4.7 and Alibaba’s Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B. These models represent a transition from standard chatbots toward "agentic" systems designed for autonomous, multi-step tasks in data-heavy environments like software engineering and finance.

New Capabilities

Claude Opus 4.7 introduces better vision, allowing it to map the precise coordinates of UI elements. It can navigate a Bloomberg terminal or complex IDE just like a human. Meanwhile, the latest Qwen 3.6-35B-A3B introduces "Thinking Preservation." Unlike predecessors, it retains reasoning traces across multiple turns, preventing the model from losing its "train of thought" during long debugging sessions or complex financial audits. This is paired with enhanced "Spatial Intelligence" for pinpoint object detection within dense graphical interfaces.

The Evolution of the Benchmark

These advances have rendered old tests like MMLU obsolete. Evaluation is shifting toward functional benchmarks like SWE-bench Verified for coding. Notably, Claude Opus 4.7 has set a new bar in professional workflows, scoring 64.4% on the Finance Agent v1.1 benchmark—outperforming competitors in long-context financial research and decision support.

However, these "Thinking Modes" demand massive compute. We are now seeing the rise of Sustainability Benchmarks, which track energy intensity and water consumption per query. In 2026, a model’s value isn't just its IQ, but its "insight-per-watt." For the data-conscious engineer, the smartest AI is now the one that scales without an unsustainable carbon footprint.

AI Products

What Is NotebookLM? Google's AI Research Tool, Explained

Raphael Ongleo  · Published May 13, 2026  · 2 min read

NotebookLM is Google's AI-powered research and knowledge management tool, and it's one of the more distinctively useful AI products out there.

NotebookLM (the LM stands for "Language Model") is a research and note-taking online tool developed by Google Labs that uses Google's Gemini AI to help users interact with their own documents.

Unlike ChatGPT or Claude, which draw on broad training data, NotebookLM only answers questions based on the sources you upload - making it highly accurate and trustworthy for research. It doesn't hallucinate in the traditional sense because it only uses your uploaded documents, and every answer includes citations linking back to source material. You can upload PDFs, website links, YouTube videos, Google Drive documents, and manually pasted text to create a centralized research hub. Each notebook can hold up to 50 sources on the free tier.

Standout features

Audio Overviews - Released in September 2024, this converts your documents into a conversational, podcast-like discussion between two AI hosts. Google later expanded this to more than 80 languages.
Video Overviews - Transforms document summaries into visual slide-style videos combining AI narration, images, diagrams, and structured explanations.
Studio outputs - You can visualize your material as a slide deck, infographic, or report, all grounded in your source documents rather than pulling from the web.
Deep Research - A "Fast Research" mode surfaces around 10 relevant documents in under 30 seconds, while the Deep Research option more slowly gathers many more sources.

AI Research

AI is evolving from a tool into a collaborator—and current research is exposing both the limits of today’s systems and the direction of what comes next.

Jude Teves  · Published May 13, 2026  · 3 min read

New work from Anthropic moves AI agent ethics from abstract principles into operational design. Instead of re‑arguing why trustworthiness matters, the research shows how autonomous agents already plan, decide, and act across systems with limited human oversight—and where real failure modes emerge. The value‑add is pragmatic: a concrete framework for building trustworthy agents that embeds human control, transparency, security, alignment, and privacy directly into system architecture. In short, as AI begins to collaborate with humans rather than merely respond to them, trust becomes a design choice, not a policy afterthought.

In parallel, research highlighted by Yann LeCun points to “world model” AI as a potential next frontier beyond large language models. While LLMs are powerful pattern recognizers, they are fundamentally trained to predict text and lack a grounded understanding of how the world behaves. World models aim to address this limitation by enabling AI systems to learn cause‑and‑effect, physical constraints, and persistent state. Early results—such as robots learning hundreds of real‑world tasks from minimal demonstration—suggest a path toward AI that can reason about environments, infrastructure, and systems, not just language.

Why it matters now: Together, these developments signal a shift toward AI as an active collaborator—capable of taking initiative and operating in real‑world contexts. The novelty is not the ethics discussion itself, but mounting evidence that LLM‑only approaches have structural limits, and that more autonomous, world‑aware AI is approaching practical use. This brings questions of governance, safeguards, and institutional readiness into the present, not the future.
